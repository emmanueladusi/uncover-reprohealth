02

Three conditions worth knowing about.

Students told us these three come up again and again — and often go undiagnosed for years.

◆

Endometriosis

Tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus, causing inflammation and pain that's often dismissed as "bad cramps" — it can affect more than the reproductive system, including energy, mood, and digestion.

Watch for: pain that disrupts your day, pain that doesn't ease with rest or OTC medication, symptoms that often start soon after your first period.

◆

PCOS

Polycystic ovary syndrome is a hormone condition that can affect periods, skin, weight, and mood. It's common — and commonly missed until symptoms are dismissed as "normal teen stuff."

Watch for: irregular or missing periods, new acne or hair growth changes, sudden weight shifts.

◆

Fibroids

Non-cancerous growths in or around the uterus. Less common in teens but not impossible — and worth knowing about, especially with a family history.

Watch for: heavy or prolonged bleeding, pelvic pressure or pain, frequent urination.

Myths that delay diagnosis.

"You're just trying to get out of class."

Fatigue and pain genuinely make it hard to concentrate — and for most people with endometriosis, pain doesn't actually improve with over-the-counter medication.

"It only affects adults, not teens."

Symptoms typically begin in adolescence, often right after a first period, and can worsen over time if untreated.

"You don't have heavy bleeding, so it can't be endo."

There's no single "required" symptom — the presence of any of these patterns is worth a conversation, not a checklist to fully match.

"Pregnancy cures it."

Pregnancy doesn't cure endometriosis — it may temporarily ease symptoms for some, due to hormone changes, but that's not the same as treatment.

This is general education, not medical advice — symptoms vary a lot between people. A doctor is the only one who can diagnose any of these.

06

Chronic pain affects more than your body.

Living with unexplained pain, or waiting years for a diagnosis, takes a toll. That's real — and support for it matters too.

🧠

You're allowed to be frustrated

Feeling dismissed by doctors, teachers, or even friends is exhausting. That frustration is a reasonable response, not an overreaction.

💛

Coping isn't fixing

Heat, rest, movement, and talking to someone you trust won't cure a condition — but they can make hard days more bearable while you get answers.

🗣️

Find your person

A school counselor, nurse, trusted teacher, or older sibling — pick one person to loop in so you're not carrying this alone.

Strategies people use to cope, day to day

  • Heat therapy
  • Anti-inflammatory eating
  • Stress reduction (meditation, journaling)
  • TENS (electrical stimulation)
  • Pelvic floor physical therapy
  • Counseling or peer support

08

How to actually get help.

At school+
Most schools have a nurse, counselor, or a trusted staff member you can talk to privately. You don't need to explain everything — "I've been having health stuff going on" is enough to start.
Before a doctor's appointment+
Write down your symptoms, when they started, and how they affect your daily life before you go in. It's easy to forget details under pressure — a list helps you (and your doctor) take it seriously.
If you feel dismissed+
You're allowed to ask questions, request a second opinion, or bring a parent or guardian into the room. "This doesn't feel resolved to me" is a completely valid thing to say.
If you don't have a trusted adult at home+
A school nurse or counselor can often connect you with community health resources. See our resource directory for options that don't require a parent to access.
