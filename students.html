Did you know?

Many reproductive health conditions first develop during adolescence.

Severe menstrual pain that prevents you from attending school, sports, or everyday activities is not something you should simply ignore.

Everyone's experience with reproductive illnesses is different — two people with the same condition may have very different symptoms.

Learning about your body helps you communicate more confidently with trusted adults and healthcare providers.

02

Five conditions worth knowing about.

Students told us these come up again and again — and often go undiagnosed for years.

◆

Endometriosis

Tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus, causing inflammation and pain that's often dismissed as "bad cramps."

Symptoms: severe cramps, chronic pelvic pain, pain during/after sex, painful bowel movements, heavy bleeding, bloating, fertility problems.

Treatment: pain medication, hormonal birth control, hormone therapy, surgery if needed.

◆

PMOS (formerly PCOS)

A hormonal condition that affects ovulation and insulin regulation. It's common — and commonly missed until symptoms are dismissed as "normal teen stuff."

Symptoms: irregular or absent periods, acne, facial/body hair growth, weight gain, hair thinning, fatigue, dark skin patches, fertility challenges.

Treatment: healthy lifestyle changes, hormonal birth control, insulin-resistance medication, regular monitoring.

◆

Fibroids

Non-cancerous growths that develop in the uterus. Less common in teens but not impossible — and worth knowing about, especially with a family history.

Symptoms: heavy periods, long cycles, pelvic pressure, frequent urination, constipation, lower back pain, pain during sex.

Treatment: medication, pain relief, myomectomy, uterine artery embolization, hysterectomy in severe cases.

◆

Adenomyosis

Similar to endometriosis, but the tissue grows into the muscular wall of the uterus itself, rather than outside it. Often causes heavy bleeding and severe, "knifelike" cramps.

Symptoms: severe menstrual cramps, abdominal pressure and bloating, heavy prolonged bleeding with large clots, painful sex.

Diagnosis: imaging (ultrasound or MRI) plus symptoms and a pelvic exam — no surgery required to diagnose it.

◆

PMDD

Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder is a much more severe form of PMS — a serious, chronic condition, not just "bad moods before your period."

Symptoms: depressed mood, intense anxiety, mood swings, irritability, trouble concentrating, fatigue, appetite/sleep changes — severe enough to disrupt daily life.

Treatment: diet changes, exercise, stress management, sometimes therapy or medication.

Myths that delay diagnosis.

"You're just trying to get out of class."

Fatigue and pain genuinely make it hard to concentrate — and for most people with endometriosis, pain doesn't actually improve with over-the-counter medication.

"Only adults get reproductive health conditions."

Many conditions begin during adolescence, often right after a first period, and can worsen over time if untreated.

"You don't have heavy bleeding, so it can't be endo."

There's no single "required" symptom — the presence of any of these patterns is worth a conversation, not a checklist to fully match.

"Fibroids are cancerous tumors."

Fibroids are non-cancerous growths. They can still cause real symptoms worth treating, but they aren't cancer.

This is general education, not medical advice — symptoms vary a lot between people. A doctor is the only one who can diagnose any of these.

04

Explore the body.

Ever wondered what your ovaries actually do, or why hormones affect your whole body, not just your cycle? Open each one below.

Brain+
Your brain (specifically the hypothalamus and pituitary gland) sends the hormone signals that kick off your whole menstrual cycle. It's why stress, sleep, and mental health can genuinely shift your cycle too.
Ovaries+
Two small organs that store eggs and release one during ovulation, roughly midway through your cycle. They also produce estrogen and progesterone — the hormones behind most of your cycle's symptoms. This is where PMOS and ovarian cysts show up.
Fallopian tubes+
The pathway an egg travels from an ovary to the uterus, and where fertilization happens if sperm is present.
Uterus+
Builds up a lining each cycle in case of pregnancy, then sheds it as your period if pregnancy doesn't happen. This is where fibroids, adenomyosis, and endometriosis-related pain often center.
Pelvic floor+
The muscles that support your pelvic organs. They can tense up in response to chronic pain, which is part of why conditions like endometriosis can cause pain beyond just "period cramps" — and why pelvic floor physical therapy is a real treatment option.
Bladder & bowel+
Sitting right next to the uterus and ovaries, these can be affected when endometriosis tissue grows nearby — which is why pain during urination or bowel movements around your period is worth mentioning to a doctor, not something to just push through.

07

Chronic pain affects more than your body.

Living with unexplained pain, or waiting years for a diagnosis, takes a toll. You may experience anxiety, depression, stress, isolation, or fatigue — that's real, and support for it matters too.

🧠

You're allowed to be frustrated

Feeling dismissed by doctors, teachers, or even friends is exhausting. That frustration is a reasonable response, not an overreaction.

💛

Coping isn't fixing

Heat, rest, movement, and talking to someone you trust won't cure a condition — but they can make hard days more bearable while you get answers.

🗣️

Find your person

A school counselor, nurse, trusted teacher, or older sibling — pick one person to loop in so you're not carrying this alone.

Healthy coping strategies

  • Talking with someone you trust
  • Joining a support group
  • Gentle exercise
  • Journaling
  • Mindfulness
  • Counselling
  • Getting enough sleep

08

How to actually get help.

If you think something isn't normal: track your symptoms, talk to a trusted adult, visit your doctor or nurse practitioner, ask for a referral if symptoms continue, and seek a second opinion if you feel dismissed. Pain that regularly keeps you from school, sports, or daily life is not something you should simply "live with."

At school+
Most schools have a nurse, counselor, or a trusted staff member you can talk to privately. You don't need to explain everything — "I've been having health stuff going on" is enough to start.
Before a doctor's appointment+
Track your symptoms using a period app, journal, or our pain tracker. It's easy to forget details under pressure — a list helps you (and your doctor) take it seriously.
If you feel dismissed+
You're allowed to ask questions, request a referral to a gynecologist, or seek a second opinion. "This doesn't feel resolved to me" is a completely valid thing to say.
If you don't have a trusted adult at home+
A school nurse or counselor can often connect you with community health resources. See our resource directory for options that don't require a parent to access.
