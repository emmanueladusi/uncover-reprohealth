01

For parents.

You don't need medical training — you need to know what to watch for and when to act.

👀

Signs to watch for

Missed school around the same time each month, avoiding activities, visible pain, or mentioning symptoms being dismissed elsewhere.

🤍

How to support

Believe them first. Ask open questions instead of minimizing ("it's just cramps"). Offer to go to appointments with them, or let them go alone if they'd rather.

⏱

When to seek help

If pain regularly disrupts school or sleep, if bleeding is heavy or prolonged, or if your child specifically asks to see a doctor about it.

🗣️

Talking about periods

Start before the first period, keep it factual and low-drama, and revisit the conversation as things change — it's not a one-time talk.

💭

Mental health

Chronic pain and dismissal affect mood, sleep, and school performance. Emotional support is as important as the physical follow-up.

🏫

School advocacy

You can request flexibility around attendance or PE without disclosing full medical details — a note to the school nurse or counselor is often enough.

02

Partners & friends.

Chronic illness is often invisible. Here's how to actually help.

✕

Things not to say

"But you don't look sick." "Have you tried just relaxing?" "Is it really that bad?" — these minimize real pain, even when well-intentioned.

✓

Helpful things to say

"What do you need right now?" "I believe you." "Do you want company, or space?" — offering options beats guessing.

🔥

Supporting flare-ups

Have a plan ahead of time: who cancels plans, what helps (heat, quiet, medication), and how much check-in they actually want.

🩺

Helping during appointments

Offer to take notes or ask the follow-up questions they're too exhausted to think of — not to speak over them.

Understanding invisible illnesses: someone can look completely fine and still be in significant pain. Take their word for it.
