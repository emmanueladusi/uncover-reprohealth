Understanding student symptoms & warning signs.

03

Anti-racist, age-aware teaching practices.

Research on healthcare inequities shows pain is believed differently depending on a student's race, gender, and age. That bias can show up in classrooms too — here's how to interrupt it.

01

Believe first, verify later

Take reported pain and discomfort seriously in the moment. You can loop in a nurse or parent afterward — don't require proof up front.

02

Watch your language

Avoid framing pain tolerance as a personality trait ("she's just dramatic," "he's tough, he's fine"). Language like this tracks along race and gender lines.

03

Age-appropriate, not watered-down

Middle schoolers can handle accurate information delivered simply. Vague answers create more anxiety than clear, calm ones.

04

Inclusive classroom discussion guides.

Frameworks for handling live classroom moments — not just lecture content.

Opening the topic without singling anyone out+
Frame lessons around "bodies" and "people who menstruate" rather than assuming every student in the room has the same experience. Offer an anonymous question box (index card or digital) so students can ask without speaking up.
Handling a disruptive or dismissive comment+
Name it calmly and move on: "We take health questions seriously in here — let's keep it respectful." Address anything serious privately afterward rather than escalating in front of the class.
When a student shares something personal+
Thank them briefly, keep it general for the rest of the class, and follow up with them privately. Never use a student's disclosure as the class's main example.
Talking about race and healthcare inequities+
It's appropriate to name, in age-appropriate terms, that studies show pain in women and girls of color is often under-treated. Frame it as a system problem to be aware of, not a scare tactic.

06

Reflection tools & case studies.

Short scenarios to think through, alone or with your department.

Case study: the "frequent absence"

A student has missed first period four times this month, always on the same week of her cycle. She hasn't said anything. What's a low-pressure way to open a conversation, and who else should be looped in?

Case study: the dismissed comment

During a health unit, a student says loudly, "my mom says period pain is just an excuse." Another student visibly reacts. How do you respond in the moment, and afterward?

ⓘ These are discussion prompts for staff development, not scripts — the goal is building comfort with judgment calls, not memorizing a "right" answer.
