The research behind this project.

This site began as a Youth Participatory Action Research (YPAR) project. Here's the question it asked, what the literature found, and where the recommendations came from.

01

The research question.

This project examines reproductive illnesses, healthcare inequities, and educational gaps by gathering anonymous testimonies from students, teachers, and individuals with lived experience — focused on the systemic barriers affecting Black and racialized teenage girls regarding conditions like endometriosis, PMOS, and fibroids.

Most existing reproductive health research centers adult women rather than adolescents aged 12–19, leaving teenage girls underrepresented in medical knowledge and policy discussions. This project set out to understand how that under-research reflects larger systemic issues within Canada's healthcare and education systems — and to build something that responds to it directly.

02

Key findings from the literature.

01

Systemic racism & gender bias in healthcare

Research shows reproductive illness in Black women and girls is often minimized, misdiagnosed, or dismissed. Race and ethnicity have been shown to influence diagnostic rates and access to quality reproductive healthcare in Ontario.

02

Diagnostic delay & dismissal

A recurring theme: teenage girls' pain being normalized or dismissed as something that will "go away." Without confidence or support to self-advocate, reproductive illnesses often go undiagnosed for years — leading to chronic pain, emotional distress, and missed school.

03

Intersection of racism, sexism & ageism

Teenage girls are often viewed as "too young" for a reproductive illness diagnosis, or assumed to be exaggerating due to hormones. Add race, and Black girls face even greater dismissal — a gap worsened by the lack of race-based healthcare data.

04

A lack of youth-centered research

Most studies focus on adult women, leaving significant gaps in understanding how reproductive illnesses affect girls aged 12–19 — limiting early diagnosis, excluding teens from policy discussions, and leaving healthcare systems unprepared to support youth.

A framework for change

Reproductive justice centers race, age, gender, lived experience, and culturally safe care — shifting healthcare from treating symptoms to systemic transformation.

03

Recommendations from the research.

Improve reproductive health education in schools, beginning in middle school.

Expand race-based and youth-centered healthcare data collection.

Include youth voices in reproductive health research and policy development.

Train healthcare professionals in anti-racist, age-aware reproductive care.

Use culturally safe, trauma-informed, participatory research methods.

Increase awareness of reproductive illnesses within schools and communities.

This website — its student hub, teacher training modules, and self-advocacy toolkit — is a direct response to these recommendations.

Sources.

Berthelot-Raffard et al. — "Research shines light on health-care inequalities for Black women" (YorkU, 2023)+
Explores reproductive health experiences — including endometriosis — in Black women within the Quebec/Canadian system. Read it at yorku.ca
Bougie, Nwosu & Warshafsky — "Revisiting the impact of race/ethnicity in endometriosis"+
Reviews race-based bias in endometriosis diagnosis and care in Ontario. Read it at researchgate.net
Coen-Sanchez, Idriss-Wheeler et al. — "Reproductive justice in patient care"+
Argues for a reproductive justice paradigm in Canada's sexual and reproductive health research. Read it at biomedcentral.com
Dr. Onye Nnorom — "Time to dismantle systemic anti-Black racism in medicine in Canada" (CMAJ)+
A critique of Canadian medical research structures, relevant to disparities in attention to women's health. Read it at cmaj.ca
Siddiqi & Lofters — "Transforming race-based health research in Canada" (CMAJ)+
Argues for systemic changes to how race and intersectionality are integrated into public health studies. Read it at cmaj.ca
Idriss-Wheeler, El-Mowafi, Coen-Sanchez, Yalahow & Yaya — "Looking through the lens of reproductive justice"+
Advocates centering reproductive justice — addressing racism, colonialism, and systemic inequities — in Canadian SRHR research. Read it at biomedcentral.com
Stephanie Awotwi-Pratt — "Navigating medical racism: How Black Canadian women experience reproductive healthcare in Canada"+
Explores lived experiences of Black Canadian women within the reproductive healthcare system, centering their voices to highlight medical racism.

Additional grounding sources: Government of Canada (endometriosis), Women's College Hospital (Pelvic Health), the Ontario Health Curriculum, and the TDSB Equity Strategy.

05

Meet the researcher.

Hi, I'm Elyse Fisher-Uriarte. I created this website as part of a Youth Participatory Action Research (YPAR) project through the Toronto District School Board's Black Student Summer Leadership Program (BSSLP).

Living with endometriosis inspired me to explore why reproductive illnesses are often misunderstood, under-researched, and overlooked — especially among teenagers and racialized communities. Through research, anonymous testimonies, and educational resource development, I hope this platform helps young people feel seen, informed, and empowered to advocate for their health.

This initiative emphasizes reproductive justice frameworks centering community knowledge, trauma-informed practices, and culturally responsive education.
