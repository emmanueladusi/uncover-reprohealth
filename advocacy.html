Trackers.

The single most useful thing you can bring to an appointment is specifics, not a vague "it's been bad."

📍

Pain tracker

Map where it hurts, when, how it affects your day, and what helps — then copy a plain-text summary for your notes app.

Open the pain tracker →
📅

Period & medication tracker

A dedicated app (or a plain calendar) works well: mark day one of your period, note duration, and log any medication you take and whether it helped. Bring the pattern, not just today's snapshot, to your appointment.

02

Appointment planner.

Check these off before you go in — five minutes of prep changes how seriously an appointment gets taken.

03

Questions to ask your doctor.

"What could be causing this, and how do we find out?"

"Is this something I should track and follow up on, or address now?"

"What are all my treatment options, not just the first one?"

"Can you refer me to a specialist if this doesn't improve?"

"What symptoms would mean I should come back sooner?"

"Can we write down a plan so I remember what we discussed?"

04

Asking for school accommodations.

You don't need a full diagnosis to ask for reasonable flexibility — a doctor's note describing "chronic pain" or "a medical condition requiring flexibility" is often enough.

05

Medical terminology.

Words that come up a lot in reproductive health appointments.

Laparoscopy+
A minor surgery using a small camera to look inside the pelvis — currently the only way to confirm an endometriosis diagnosis with certainty.
Transvaginal ultrasound+
An imaging test using a wand-shaped scanner to get a clearer picture of the uterus and ovaries than an external ultrasound.
GnRH agonist+
A type of medication that temporarily pauses your cycle and lowers estrogen — sometimes used to manage endometriosis or adenomyosis pain.
Menorrhagia+
The medical term for abnormally heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding.
Referral+
When your regular doctor sends you to see a specialist, like a gynecologist, for something outside their scope.
Hormonal IUD+
A small device placed in the uterus that releases hormones to reduce bleeding and pain — one option for managing several reproductive conditions, not just contraception.
